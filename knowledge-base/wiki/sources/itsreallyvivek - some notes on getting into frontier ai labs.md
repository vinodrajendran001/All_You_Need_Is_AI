# itsreallyvivek - some notes on getting into frontier ai labs

## Summary

A short essay-thread arguing that frontier AI labs do not really select for "research" and "trench engineering" as separate skills. The deeper common ability is to build useful abstractions when no complete map exists. The essay's most durable value for this vault is that it sharpens the existing [[Automated AI Research]] page: execution is not the hardest bottleneck; judgment, abstraction, and choosing the right anomaly or question are.

## Key claims

- Frontier AI labs operate in a regime where **nobody knows the answer** in advance. That makes progress depend less on recovering known solutions and more on acting under uncertainty.
- Research matters not because it produces papers, but because it trains the ability to **form hypotheses, confront reality, update beliefs, and continue without a map**.
- The deeper skill in large-scale engineering is not memorizing ever more detail. It is **compression**: constructing a useful abstraction that predicts how a complex system behaves.
- Great engineers do not search every possibility. They use causal abstractions to eliminate most possibilities quickly and focus on the few that matter.
- Research and engineering therefore converge:
  - **research is compression of uncertainty**
  - **engineering is compression of complexity**
- The scarce resource in frontier work is not information but **judgment**: deciding which question, bottleneck, or anomaly actually deserves attention.
- The essay's strongest line for this vault is that frontier AI is increasingly a race for **better abstractions**, not just more raw intelligence.

## Why it matters

This source deepens [[Automated AI Research]] by giving a sharper language for the current bottleneck: the hard part is not only executing experiments, but choosing which experiments are worth running and building abstractions that reduce a huge search space to a tractable one.

## Tensions / open questions

- The source is an essay-thread, not a technical paper, so its value is conceptual rather than empirical.
- It frames abstraction and taste as scarce human skills, which raises a natural follow-up question for this vault: to what extent can agent systems learn or simulate that judgment?
