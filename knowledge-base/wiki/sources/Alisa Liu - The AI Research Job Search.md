# Alisa Liu - The AI Research Job Search

## Summary

A first-person field report on the Research Scientist / Member of Technical Staff job search at the end of a 6-year NLP PhD (University of Washington, tokenization focus). It demystifies the process with one concrete data point: 11 companies, 57 interviews, 46 recruiter calls, 16 post-offer chats. The post's durable value is a clean **taxonomy of interview types**, a **preparation methodology**, and candid notes on **timing and negotiation** — making it the vault's most complete account of how to prepare for ML research interviews end to end.

It is also the parent document for two companion study references the author built during the search: [[Alisa Liu - Book of LLMs]] and [[Alisa Liu - Math Notes]].

## Key claims

- **Technical skills and knowledge are evaluated much more than research experience** — though research experience is usually what gets you the interview in the first place.
- **Seven interview types** recur: (1) **ML coding** — by far the most common; implement an architecture, a decoding strategy, or a classic ML algorithm; PyTorch fluency is a must, occasionally pure numpy for a from-scratch backward pass; (2) **general coding** — LeetCode-style; (3) **technical discussion** — no coding but technical, either an extended experiment-design discussion (tests *how you think*) or rapid-fire breadth questions (tests *what you know*); (4) **research discussion** — narrate a past project and defend choices; (5) **behavioral** — textbook STAR, pre-map PhD stories to questions; (6) **math** — probability, linear algebra, calculus, from logic puzzles to pen-and-paper derivations; (7) **job talk** — shorter than academic, focused on one paper/direction.
- **"There is truly no better use of your time than studying for interviews."** The job search is a full-time job; technical interviews require dedicated effort beyond doing research.
- **A concrete prep path:** start with Stanford **CS336 (Language Modeling from Scratch)** to map the field, then deep-dive concepts one at a time (papers, blogs, talking to ChatGPT/Claude, implementing from scratch). **CS336 Homework 1 (implement/debug a transformer) should become muscle memory.** **Practice with AI assistance fully off** to mimic interview conditions.
- **Each interview is its own cram session:** "a slightly different math or CS class, you never went to lectures, and now you have ~3 days to cram for the midterm." Swap the most relevant knowledge into your head per interview.
- **Sleep beats last-minute cramming** on interview day; record notes afterward.
- **Studying has large side benefits:** broader breadth improved research confidence and made the author "enormously more effective" on the ongoing project.
- **Negotiation is a separate, hard skill** that cannot be solved by studying. Initial offers are designed to be negotiated; a few weeks of effort can equal years of work at the initial offer. Lean on friends for calibration; pre-script what you will/won't share.
- **Timing/logistics wisdom:** use a few companies for practice but guard stamina; company headcount and active-hiring teams can matter more than prep; most deadlines are flexible but beware "exploding" offers.

## Tensions / open questions

- One person's data point from a specific slice (NLP PhD → research-scientist roles, 2026 market); generalization to MLE/SWE roles, non-PhD candidates, or other years is partial.
- The emphasis "technical knowledge > research experience" reflects the interview *loop*, and sits in productive tension with [[itsreallyvivek - some notes on getting into frontier ai labs]], which argues the deeper selected-for skill is judgment/abstraction under uncertainty.
- Negotiation advice is directional, not a script; outcomes depend heavily on leverage and market.
